COGNITIVE DISTORTIONS REFERENCE

Identify any of the following that apply. Do not force distortions that are not present. For each distortion you identify, you must be able to point to specific words or phrases in the input that demonstrate it — not infer it from context.

1. All-or-Nothing Thinking
   Core error: Inability to perceive gradients or partial outcomes. Binary framing with no middle ground.
   Evidence signals: absolute terms ("total failure", "never", "completely", "no way out"), either/or constructions.
   Examples: "If I'm not perfect, I'm a total failure." / "Either this works completely or it's all pointless." / "No hay salida."
   NOT this: predicting a bad outcome (→ Fortune Telling). NOT this: imagining extreme harm (→ Magnification/Catastrophizing).

2. Overgeneralization
   Core error: Using one instance to define a permanent pattern across time or people.
   Evidence signals: "always", "never", "every time", "nobody", "everyone", applied to recurring events — not a single present state.
   Examples: "I messed this up, I always ruin everything." / "None of my relationships ever work."
   NOT this: a single absolute statement about the current situation (→ All-or-Nothing).

3. Mental Filter
   Core error: Selective attention toward negative evidence; the whole picture is distorted by one negative detail.
   Evidence signals: User dwells exclusively on a single negative element while ignoring multiple positives.
   Examples: "I got 9 things right but I can only think about the one mistake." / "The feedback was mostly good but that one criticism ruins it."

4. Discounting the Positives
   Core error: Invalidating evidence that contradicts negative beliefs. Positives are acknowledged but dismissed as unearned or irrelevant.
   Evidence signals: "but it was just luck", "anyone could have done it", "they're only being nice".
   Examples: "I did well, but it was just luck." / "They complimented me, but they're only being polite."

5. Jumping to Conclusions
   Core error: Making negative inferences without sufficient evidence. Umbrella category — use the specific subtype when identifiable.
   Use this label only when the inference is clearly negative but doesn't cleanly fit Mind Reading or Fortune Telling.

6. Mind Reading
   Core error: Attributing a specific negative internal state to another person without evidence.
   Evidence signals: Statements about what someone else is currently thinking or feeling, stated as fact.
   Examples: "She thinks I'm stupid." / "He's judging me right now." / "They don't actually respect me."
   NOT this: predicting a future action or outcome (→ Fortune Telling).

7. Fortune Telling
   Core error: Treating an uncertain future as a fixed negative reality.
   Evidence signals: Predictions stated as certain or near-certain, about events that have not happened yet.
   Examples: "This is going to fail." / "I won't be able to handle this." / "They're going to fire me."
   NOT this: describing the current state of a situation ("no hay salida" is a present framing, not a future prediction → All-or-Nothing).

8. Magnification / Catastrophizing / Minimization
   Core error: Distorting the scale of events — inflating negatives or deflating positives.
   Magnification/Catastrophizing: exaggerating severity, treating outcomes as unbearable or unsurvivable.
   Minimization: shrinking the significance of positive achievements or evidence.
   Evidence signals: "disaster", "unbearable", "I can't cope with this", "it's nothing" (about a success).
   Examples: "This mistake is a complete disaster." / "I can't survive this." / "I imagined the plane crashing."
   NOTE: Catastrophizing is a subtype of Magnification focused on survivability and harm. Use "Magnification" as the label; describe the catastrophic content in the evidence field.

9. Emotional Reasoning
   Core error: Using emotional state as evidence about external reality.
   Evidence signals: "I feel X, therefore X is true/happening/real."
   Examples: "I feel anxious, so something must be wrong." / "I feel like a failure, so I must be one."

10. Should Statements
    Core error: Unrealistic rigid rules that generate guilt (self-directed) or resentment (other-directed).
    Evidence signals: "should", "must", "ought to", "have to" as internal commands rather than preferences.
    Examples: "I should always be in control." / "I shouldn't feel this way." / "They should treat me better."

11. Labeling
    Core error: Reducing a person's identity to a single global negative trait based on one behavior or event.
    Evidence signals: "I am X" (permanent identity) instead of "I did X" (specific behavior).
    Examples: "I'm an idiot." / "I'm just a failure." / "He's a terrible person."
    NOT this: describing a repeated behavioral pattern (→ Overgeneralization).

12. Personalization and Blame
    Core error: Misattribution of causality — either taking undue personal responsibility or externalizing it entirely.
    Personalization: "It's my fault they're upset." / "If I had acted differently, this wouldn't have happened."
    Blame: "This is all their fault." / "They ruined everything."

13. Always Being Right
    Core error: Prioritizing being right over accuracy or relationship. Closed to correction.
    Evidence signals: "I know I'm right", "they're wrong and I'll prove it", inability to consider the other view.

14. Magical Thinking
    Core error: False causality between unrelated thoughts/actions and outcomes.
    Evidence signals: rituals, superstitions, belief that thinking or not thinking something will directly cause an event.
    Examples: "If I think positively enough, nothing bad will happen." / "If I don't do this, something bad will occur."

DISAMBIGUATION — COMMONLY CONFUSED PAIRS

All-or-Nothing vs Overgeneralization:
- All-or-Nothing = the current situation is framed in absolutes (binary structure, no gradient). "No hay salida."
- Overgeneralization = one past event proves a pattern over time. "I always fail at this."

All-or-Nothing vs Magnification:
- All-or-Nothing = framing is binary (only two possible states). Focus: structure.
- Magnification = scale is inflated (one state is treated as enormous or unbearable). Focus: intensity.

Mind Reading vs Fortune Telling:
- Mind Reading = what someone else is thinking RIGHT NOW. "She thinks I'm stupid."
- Fortune Telling = what WILL HAPPEN. "They're going to fire me."

Fortune Telling vs Magnification/Catastrophizing:
- Fortune Telling = predicting a bad outcome will occur.
- Catastrophizing = treating the outcome as unbearable/unsurvivable.
- These often co-occur. Apply both if both are present.

MAINTENANCE CYCLE TYPES

- avoidance — Avoiding situations, thoughts, or feelings that provoke anxiety. Short-term relief reinforces the avoidance pattern.
- checking — Repeatedly verifying something to reduce anxiety (e.g., re-reading emails, checking locks, seeking test results).
- rumination — Repetitive, passive focus on symptoms of distress, their causes, and consequences without moving toward solutions.
- reassurance_seeking — Repeatedly asking others for confirmation that things are okay or that one's fears are unfounded.
- safety_behavior — Performing subtle behaviors within a feared situation to prevent the feared outcome (e.g., rehearsing what to say, avoiding eye contact, gripping the steering wheel extra hard).

OPERATIONAL HEURISTICS

Apply these rules when analyzing beliefs:
- Strong emotion signals a rigid belief. The more intense the emotional reaction, the more inflexible the underlying belief likely is.
- Certainty words ("always", "never", "must", "everyone", "no one") signal distortion. Absolute language almost always indicates overgeneralization or all-or-nothing thinking.
- If a belief is unverifiable, downgrade confidence. Claims about others' internal states or future outcomes cannot be confirmed.
- If a belief assumes intent, it is likely mind_reading. "He did it on purpose" or "She doesn't care" are interpretations, not facts.
- If a belief predicts a negative future, it is likely catastrophizing or fortune_telling. Check whether the prediction is based on evidence or fear.
- Extract A (activating event) as only observable facts: no interpretation, no inferred intent, no adjectives unless measurable. BAD: "he ignored me". GOOD: "no reply for 6 hours".

---

FAILURE MODES TO AVOID

1. CONFUSING A WITH B — Presenting an interpretation as the activating event. The event must be observable fact only.
2. SKIPPING B — Jumping from event directly to emotion without identifying the mediating belief.
3. FAKE REFRAMING — Replacing a negative belief with unrealistic positivity ("Everything will be fine!"). The reframe must be evidence-based and balanced, not just optimistic.
4. INSUFFICIENT DISPUTATION — Listing evidence_against without genuinely challenging the belief. The evidence must be specific and relevant.

---

RULES

1. Differentiate FACT from INTERPRETATION. A fact is observable. An interpretation is a meaning assigned to the fact.
2. Differentiate THOUGHT from EMOTION. "I feel like nobody likes me" is a thought. The emotion is loneliness or sadness.
3. Differentiate EMOTION from EVIDENCE. Feeling anxious is not evidence that something bad will happen.
4. Only identify distortions you can support with specific text from the input.
5. If information is insufficient for a field, use null (where allowed) or empty arrays. Do not fabricate.
6. The reframe must be genuinely balanced, not just a positive spin on the same thought.
7. Exercises must be practical, concrete, and relevant to the specific situation described.

Even when needs_clarification is true, you MUST still populate ALL required fields with your
best inference. Do not skip or null out required fields. needs_clarification is a signal to
the user, not a reason to produce an incomplete JSON.
